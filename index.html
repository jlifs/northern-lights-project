What causes this phenomenon?

Aurora Borealis

The Northern Lights, also known as the aurora borealis, are a natural light display in the night sky, primarily visible near Earth's magnetic poles. They occur when charged particles from the sun collide with the Earth's atmosphere, causing the gases to glow in various colors.

Best places to see the lights

Tromsø, Norway

Tromsø, Norway is a prime location for seeing the Northern Lights due to its geographical position within the Northern Lights oval. The city is situated directly below this oval, a continuous ring of auroral activity, making it a hotspot for witnessing the lights.

Best time

From late August to early April, with peak visibility between December and February during the Polar Night when the city is shrouded in almost continuous darkness.

Tip

To maximize your chances, stay in Tromsø for at least 3-5 days, as weather conditions can change.

Blue Lagoon, Iceland

The Blue Lagoon's location, away from the city, offers a good chance of spotting the aurora because it's less affected by light pollution than areas closer to Reykjavik.

Best time

Generally between September and April, especially during the darker months of November to January and around the equinoxes in September and March. This is because the long, dark nights of winter provide the best conditions for viewing.

Tip

It's recommended that you spend at least 7 nights in the country for the greatest odds.

Fairbanks, Alaska

Fairbanks is a prime location to witness the Northern Lights due to its ideal location within the Auroral Oval. Additionally, its low light pollution and relatively clear night skies create optimal viewing conditions.

Best time

Aurora Season in Fairbanks runs from August 21st to April 21st between 10:30 p.m. and 2:00 a.m.

Tip

Fairbanks is prone to unpredictable weather, and clear skies are essential for seeing the lights. Staying for at least 3-4 nights increases your likelihood of having at least one clear night.
